Beholder: Beauty in the eyes of Playboy

Data of Playmates reveals a fundamental change happening behind the scenes.

Playboy has always been a study in contradictions. It manages to be both a “nudie mag”, and a home for the writings of literary greats like Margaret Atwood, Kurt Vonnegut, Haruki Murakami and Joyce Carol Oates.

It’s broken boundaries and seen itself as activists, while others have described its output as setting women back by centuries.

Taking it from a kitchen in a Chicago apartment to a landmark mansion in Beverly Hills, Playboy’s founder Hugh Hefner, a figure both vilified and praised, turned it into a cultural icon that would come to influence the world for more than 65 years. Along the way, even the Supreme Court has had to contend with it.

A casualty of the COVID-19 pandemic, the physical print of the magazine ended in the spring of 2020, and in 2021 Playboy stopped picking the iconic Playmate of the Month.

Under the surface, looking at the data of Playmates reveals a more fundamental change happening behind the scenes. Slowly, quietly and without much notice - a paradigm shift. Since Hefner’s death, Playboy has been redefining how it beholds beauty.

How did Playboy view beauty over the years, and where is the trend headed now?

⚠️ There are mentions of body measurements, and some content in this article might be triggering to those who have current or past experiences with disordered eating or body dysmorphia.

Since the first issue in December 1953, Playboy picked the Playmate of the Month 812 times, featuring 806 unique women. Playmates are featured in what’s known as the “centerfold” of the magazine, where the pages have no break between them. Since 2021, the “Playmate of the Month” title is no longer awarded, and a Playmate is picked every quarter.

While we look at the data for Playmates, it’s important to remember that there are real human beings behind this data. Through the years, Playmates have been admired, but more often vilified for their work in what is essentially the sex industry. The cultural, political and religious framing around sexuality and beauty has a way of clouding judgments — and while the data points serve as a clarifying lens — we need to be careful about reducing these humans to a handful of numbers. This particular set of numbers serve as anchors so we can talk about beauty as it’s perceived by Playboy, limited as they might be in describing actual beauty.

The Broken Hourglass

The costume and the three inch high heels were not comfortable at all! The costume was so tight it cut off the circulation in my legs and at the end of the night I could barely walk because my feet were so sore.

- Sharon Rogers, about the Bunny costume she had to wear in the Chicago club.

“The Hourglass Figure” is one of the most well-known body shapes. All across the media, including Playboy, it is favored above others. Yet, there is evidence that this insistence is responsible for the widespread prevalence of mental disorders surrounding body image including body dysmorphia and disordered eating, particularly among young women. Research supports that the most commonplace efforts to change the body to conform to beauty standards do not often ultimately lead to positive body image.

The “Most Average” Playmates

We focused on how the average American woman in her 20s differs from models selected as Playmates, but what about within the group? Who is the most average Playmate, and who is the least average?

When looking at the average values across all Playmates for their weight, height, body measurements, hair color and ethnicity, and ordering them by their distance from this average, some trends are revealed.

Most/Least Average Playmates

This visualization shows models selected as Playmates ordered by their distance to the average of their data points. One trend is shown: Old-School Average vs New-School Average. The top 5 most average are all from older issues, whereas the top 5 least average are all from 2003 and later.

Individuals All

Behind all the flash and glamour that comes with earning Playmate of the Month and living in the Playboy Mansion, these women are like any of us. They went to college, struggled with drug abuse and overcame it, established philanthropic foundations, used their fortune to open restaurants and helped those in need, had insecurities, got interested in politics, grappled with depression, led quiet lives, and not so quiet lives. They were into LoL and Diablo 3, dreamed of becoming Playmates, and worked at Dairy Queen. They were victims of domestic violence, wanted to become broadcasters, were single moms that got sued by Playboy, and on and on.

They were and are real people who had a real impact on others around them, good and bad. It’s worth noting this before we go celebrity worshipping or shunning models in this industry.

One thing is clear, Hugh Hefner’s death has had more impact on who appears in Playboy than its business dealings or revenues. The real world in which Playmates lived, a reality which was masterfully hidden behind glamour shots and PR campaigns, has started to be reflected in the types of women featured as well. It has changed Playboy’s beauty standards from the core - ever so slightly, but a shift nonetheless - and this is a hopeful change.

Methodology

Measurement data was gathered from mypmates.club and babepedia. Some obvious mistakes were manually fixed by cross-checking other sources and finally compiled together. When there were disagreements about data, mypmates.club values were used.

The trendlines shown in red were computed via LOESS. Averages for women in the US between 2015-2018 are from the CDC NCHS Vital and Health Statistics.

For the “most average” calculations, the Euclidean distance from the normalized average is used across most of the data points, taking into account Height, Weight, Bust, Waist, Hips, Age, Hair Color, Ethnicity and Cosmetic Enhancements.

Data collected and processed with TS, and visualized with Preact and d3. See the code on the repository.
