Beholder: Beauty in the Eyes of Playboy

Data of Playmates reveals a fundamental change happening behind the scenes.

Playboy has always been a study in contradictions. It manages to be both a “nudie mag”, and a home for the writings of literary greats like Margaret Atwood, Kurt Vonnegut, Haruki Murakami and Joyce Carol Oates.

It’s broken boundaries and seen itself as activists, while others have described its output as setting women back by centuries.

Taking it from a kitchen in a Chicago apartment to a landmark mansion in Beverly Hills, Playboy’s founder Hugh Hefner, a figure both vilified and praised, turned it into a cultural icon that would come to influence the world for more than 65 years. Along the way, even the Supreme Court has had to contend with it.

A casualty of the COVID-19 pandemic, the physical print of the magazine ended in the spring of 2020, and in 2021 Playboy stopped picking the iconic Playmate of the Month.

Under the surface, looking at the data of Playmates reveals a more fundamental change happening behind the scenes. Slowly, quietly and without much notice - a paradigm shift. Since Hefner’s death, Playboy has been redefining how it beholds beauty.

How did Playboy view beauty over the years, and where is the trend headed now?

⚠️ There are mentions of body measurements, and some content in this article might be triggering to those who have current or past experiences with disordered eating or body dysmorphia.

Since the first issue in December 1953, Playboy picked the Playmate of the Month 812 times, featuring 806 unique women. Playmates are featured in what’s known as the “centerfold” of the magazine, where the pages have no break between them. Since 2021, the “Playmate of the Month” title is no longer awarded, and a Playmate is picked every quarter.

While we look at the data for Playmates, it’s important to remember that there are real human beings behind this data. Through the years, Playmates have been admired, but more often vilified for their work in what is essentially the sex industry. The cultural, political and religious framing around sexuality and beauty has a way of clouding judgments — and while the data points serve as a clarifying lens — we need to be careful about reducing these humans to a handful of numbers. This particular set of numbers serve as anchors so we can talk about beauty as it’s perceived by Playboy, limited as they might be in describing actual beauty.

Marilyn Monroe was the first ever “Sweetheart of the Month”, before the introduction of the Playmate of the Month title. Hugh Hefner had bought previously unpublished nude photos of her for a calendar, and picked the “sexiest” picture for the first centerfold. Although she was not the copyright holder, Ms. Monroe did not consent to those images’ use and she reportedly was not compensated.

The hype around her appearance catapulted the magazine to success right from the start.

In March of 1955, the staff couldn’t meet the deadlines to deliver the issue, so nothing was released.

Jennifer Jackson, model and now a social worker from Chicago was the first Black Playmate ever to be featured in the magazine.

This was a time at the height of the Civil Rights Movement, and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 would be introduced only months later.

Later, Playboy tweeted some of the angry letters they received after featuring a Black Playmate.

In November 2017, Ines Rau became the first openly transgender Playmate in the history of the magazine.

This was however her second appearance, her first image in May of 2014 was titled “Evolution”.

After backlash, Playboy tweeted their support for this decision with the caption: “Standing on the right side of history”.

Marsha Elle is a singer-songwriter and a motivational speaker based out of Miami. She was born with proximal femoral focal deficiency which required the amputation of her right leg, and she’s had a prosthetic leg since 5 years old. After struggling with insecurity for most of her life – she didn’t wear shorts until the age of 23! – she became the first amputee Playmate.

End of an Era

Hugh Hefner passed away on September 27, 2017. It’s reported that he had been personally involved in picking Playmates of the Month, and the data below suggests that the characteristics of who gets picked have started changing significantly after his death.

Even though it’s evident that Playboy has often been progressive, this also highlights some of the biases that he might have reinforced through his personal touch.

While Hefner had his fans, it is known that many of those who worked with him did not have a positive experience, and a lot of Playmates have spoken out against him.

Age

There has been a dramatic increase of about 4 years in the ages of Playmates since the first issue.

Note the highlighted area in red. 8 Playmates, up until 1981 were under the age of 18 when photographed. This would be a felony today. Wikipedia quotes, “in the early years of the magazine, laws regarding corruption of a minor were less well established.”

There is the interesting story of one Playmate, who was photographed at the age of 16. Hugh Hefner and the Playmate’s mother were arrested, but the case was dismissed after they found that her mother had signed a statement saying that her daughter was 18.

The names of these individuals are not included in this article.

Height

Playmates have always been taller than the average American woman, but they have gotten even taller through time, about a 5% increase since 1954.

The average height of women in the US was 5’4” between 2015-2018.

Weight

It was a competition because you couldn't gain any weight. They watched your weight, they watched your appearance, and no one paid you a salary.

- Jennifer Jackson

Weight has similarly seen an increase of about 8%. What is striking is how the average model weight sharply strays from the prior average after Hefner's death, strongly suggesting Hefner favored women of a certain, smaller size.

Ali Chanel is an obvious outlier, and a welcome one for body positivity. Dubbed “Playboy’s curviest Playmate”, she has encouraged others to accept their bodies as they are.

Bust

There has been a significant drop in bust sizes, 9.6% decrease overall. This is interesting, because most sources online claim that bust size, especially in the US, has actually gone up, but without citing clear sources. This divergence highlights yet another contradictory nature of Playboy.

Between September 2012 until Hefner’s death, there was an inexplicable upper cap on bust size. We start seeing huge variance once again, like the early days, after his death.

Waist

The most significant change among body measurements is the waist, with an 11% increase. Playmates with much larger waist measurements started being featured especially after 2017. About 13% of the women were above 28” after Hefner’s death, compared to <1% up until that time.

Even with that, the average waist measurement of women in the US was 36" between 2015–2018. If we plot it on this graph, it would extend beyond the top axis. In other words, no Playmate has ever been at the US average for waist measurement.

Having a smaller waist is the most statistically significant distinction between Playmates and the US average.

Hips

Yet another example of explosion of variance after Hefner’s death, hip measurements have seen a minor 2% increase, yet most of that increase is carried by the post-2017 Playmates with much higher measurements than in the history of the magazine.

In fact, 20% of the Playmates had larger hips than at any other point in the magazine’s history after Hefner’s death.

This minor increase suggests that while post-Hefner era Playboy favors a wider variety of models on a variety of fronts, a small waist remains standard.

Hair color

What do you think of when you think of hair color in the context of Playboy magazine? If you're like me, you think “blond”.

The truth is that there is almost equal representation of brown and blond hair, but black hair is underrepresented.

A study in 2003 reported that only 4% of adults in the US are naturally blond, and so we see a huge overrepresentation of blonds.

Clearly, having light hair has become the “beauty standard”, as 48% of White college women dyed their hair blond or blonder, higher than any other color. This might also be a reason why black hair is underrepresented.

Could the dominant cultural force of Playboy be influencing some of the choices made by college-aged women, even if indirectly? And in that vein, to what extent is Playboy an aspirational publication?

Cosmetic Enhancements

Essential to the conversation surrounding Playboy’s view of beauty is the lengths that many individuals went to achieve it. Cosmetic enhancements by way of surgical procedures are quite common among models who became Playmates.

In this case, we can clearly see that enhanced breasts came into prominence starting around 1985 and peaked around 2010s before fading away again. This is doubly interesting when you consider that bust sizes have steadily gone down over the years.

Ethnicity

Despite featuring a Black Playmates early in their publication, historically, Playboy undoubtedly prefers white women. The magazine reinforced the standard of beauty in America as largely limited to the domain of white women, with 90% of its Playmates being white. It featured a single Black woman for the first time in 1965, and it did not feature a Black woman again for four years. This pattern suggests a standard of beauty that does not represent the diversity of the country, and the stark underrepresentation is striking.

Playboy’s exclusion of BIPOC individuals from its pages suggests that their vision of beauty fits squarely within the colonizer perspective and constructs the “All American Beauty” as a blond, skinny white woman. This impacts more than just their readers, as the “blast radius” of its influence continues to negatively impact BIPOC communities, especially young women, and will likely continue to harm them for years to come.

Also, despite their progressive image, only 2019 and 2020 had 40% non-white Playmates, the most it has ever been in its 67-year history. In fact, these are the only years that reflected the actual population distribution in the United States.

The Broken Hourglass

The costume and the three inch high heels were not comfortable at all! The costume was so tight it cut off the circulation in my legs and at the end of the night I could barely walk because my feet were so sore.

- Sharon Rogers, about the Bunny costume she had to wear in the Chicago club.

“The Hourglass Figure” is one of the most well-known body shapes. All across the media, including Playboy, it is favored above others. Yet, there is evidence that this insistence is responsible for the widespread prevalence of mental disorders surrounding body image including body dysmorphia and disordered eating, particularly among young women. Research supports that the most commonplace efforts to change the body to conform to beauty standards do not often ultimately lead to positive body image.

Individuals All

Behind all the flash and glamour that comes with earning Playmate of the Month and living in the Playboy Mansion, these women are like any of us. They went to college, struggled with drug abuse and overcame it, established philanthropic foundations, used their fortune to open restaurants and helped those in need, had insecurities, got interested in politics, grappled with depression, led quiet lives, and not so quiet lives. They were into LoL and Diablo 3, dreamed of becoming Playmates, and worked at Dairy Queen. They were victims of domestic violence, wanted to become broadcasters, were single moms that got sued by Playboy, and on and on.

They were and are real people who had a real impact on others around them, good and bad. It’s worth noting this before we go celebrity worshipping or shunning models in this industry.

One thing is clear, Hugh Hefner’s death has had more impact on who appears in Playboy than its business dealings or revenues. The real world in which Playmates lived, a reality which was masterfully hidden behind glamour shots and PR campaigns, has started to be reflected in the types of women featured as well. It has changed Playboy’s beauty standards from the core - ever so slightly, but a shift nonetheless - and this is a hopeful change.

Methodology

Measurement data was gathered from mypmates.club and babepedia. Some obvious mistakes were manually fixed by cross-checking other sources and finally compiled together. When there were disagreements about data, mypmates.club values were used.

The trendlines shown in red were computed via LOESS. Averages for women in the US between 2015-2018 are from the CDC NCHS Vital and Health Statistics.

For the “most average” calculations, the Euclidean distance from the normalized average is used across most of the data points, taking into account Height, Weight, Bust, Waist, Hips, Age, Hair Color, Ethnicity and Cosmetic Enhancements.

Data collected and processed with TS, and visualized with Preact and d3. See the code on the repository.
